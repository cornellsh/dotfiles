# Example 3: DTC manifesto

A worked example of the skill applied to a brand launch manifesto for a fictional DTC coffee brand called "Llano."

---

## The brief from the user

> We are launching a coffee brand called Llano. The story: we source from one farm in Colombia (Hacienda Llano Verde, near Manizales) where the family has been growing for four generations. The category problem: specialty coffee marketing has become abstract, vague, full of tasting notes nobody understands. We want a manifesto on the launch page that says what we believe. The voice should sound like a person, not a brand. Long copy is fine. The page will run as the entire homepage at launch.

Inputs extracted:

- One thing: convince the reader that Llano is worth their attention for a launch order.
- Reader: specialty coffee buyer who has tried four or five brands already. Wants something that does not sound like every other one.
- Belief shift: from "specialty coffee marketing is interchangeable" to "this one is different because it is one farm and one family."
- Promise: the coffee is from one place, one family, one set of decisions every year. Implicit promise: more honest than the category.
- Proof: the farm exists; the family has photos; the harvest dates are public; the brewing recommendations are written by the farm, not by a copywriter.
- Format: manifesto, 200 to 400 words, runs as launch homepage.
- Voice: Voice 3 (manifesto) blended with Voice 8 (founder narrator). Stance, with personal specifics.
- CTA: subscribe to launch list. Quiet button at the end.

---

## The output

### Idea
The coffee category has talked itself into abstraction. We have one farm, one family, and a season that decides every year.

### Final copy

**Headline**
> We disagree with most coffee marketing.

**Body**
> Most specialty coffee comes from a blend of beans from places the brand cannot name without looking them up. The packaging reads like a wine review written by someone who has not tasted it. Notes of citrus, of jasmine, of dark chocolate.
>
> We come from one farm. Hacienda Llano Verde, near Manizales, Colombia. The family who runs it has been growing coffee on the same slope for four generations. The farm manager is Rocío, the third daughter, who came back from agronomy school in 2019 and has been deciding the harvest dates ever since.
>
> Every year is different. Last year was warmer than usual. The cherries ripened ten days early and we had to pick faster than the family had ever picked before. The coffee from that year tastes like that year. It is brighter than the year before, lower on body, higher on acid. Rocío writes the brewing card herself. If she thinks a batch is better as a pour-over than as espresso, she says so on the bag.
>
> We will not pretend the coffee tastes the same every year. We will not write a tasting note we did not taste. We will not tell you it is "smooth and balanced" when last year's cherries got picked in a hurry and the cup is bright.
>
> If you want a coffee that tastes the same every Tuesday morning, buy a coffee that comes from a blend. There are good ones. We are not one.
>
> If you want to drink what one farm did this year, sign up. We will tell you when the first bags ship.

**CTA**
> Tell me when Llano ships.
> (small email field, no second CTA, no banner)

### Alternates

1. **Open with the family instead of the disagreement.** "Rocío came back to the farm in 2019" as the first line. Story-led rather than stance-led. Use if the reader is more emotional than skeptical.
2. **Open with the single line.** Shorter manifesto, single paragraph, 80 words. Use as the hero section if the rest of the homepage carries the proof.
3. **End on the family rather than the offer.** Replace the "if you want, sign up" close with a closing line from Rocío herself. Tradeoff: lower email capture rate, higher brand memory.
4. **Add a postscript.** "PS. Manizales is at 1,800 meters. The slope of the farm matters. If you want the explanation of why, we will send it with your first bag." Tradeoff: invites another scroll past the CTA, can pull readers away from the action.

### Notes

Word count: 295 words, inside the manifesto upper bound from `formats.md`.

Framework doing the work: the manifesto structure from `craft.md`. Open with a stance ("we disagree with most coffee marketing"). Name the enemy (the category's vague tasting notes). Build the case with specifics (Rocío, 2019, 1,800 meters, ten days early). State what the brand will not do, three times in parallel. Close with a small instruction, not a button shout.

Voice craft moves applied:
- The blend of Voice 3 (manifesto) and Voice 8 (founder narrator) lets the manifesto stay grounded in a real person without becoming a personal essay.
- Specifics replace adjectives throughout: "near Manizales" not "in the mountains," "third daughter" not "young farmer," "ten days early" not "earlier than normal."
- The parallel structure ("We will not pretend... We will not write... We will not tell you...") is used once and dropped. The rule of three from `craft.md` is allowed only when the items are not interchangeable. These three are not.
- The close is small. "Tell me when Llano ships" is an instruction, not a "Join the revolution."

Self-edit pass: read aloud, no stumbles. No em dashes, no compound-word hyphens, no banned vocabulary. The claim about Rocío and 2019 is flagged in Notes as needing verification (this is a fictional brand; in a real brief, the user would confirm or correct the family details before publishing).

## Postmortem: what the skill did

The briefing pass identified the enemy first. Without an enemy, a manifesto has no shape. The category's vague tasting notes became the enemy and the rest of the manifesto wrote itself.

The voice blend (Voice 3 + Voice 8) was the key craft decision. Pure Voice 3 manifesto reads as preachy. Pure Voice 8 founder narrator reads as personal-essay-on-Medium. The blend lets the brand take a stance and back it with one specific person's hand on the farm.

The "we will not" parallel was used once and dropped. The rule of three is allowed when the items are not interchangeable. These three (pretend, write, tell you) name three different forms of category dishonesty.

The close ("Tell me when Llano ships") is the smallest possible call to action. It mirrors the manifesto's voice: a person speaking to a person. A "Join the Llano family" or "Subscribe" button would have broken the voice.

What to steal from this example: manifestos work when they name an enemy in the first three sentences and stake a position the rest of the page can defend. The voice should sound like a person you would have coffee with, not a brand you would buy from. The close should be small.
