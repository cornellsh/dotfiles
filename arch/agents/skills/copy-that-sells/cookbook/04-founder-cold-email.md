# Example 4: Founder cold email

A worked example of the skill applied to a cold outreach email from a founder to a prospective B2B customer.

---

## The brief from the user

> I'm the founder of a small product called Pulse. It's a tool for engineering managers to run async standups. I want to reach out to engineering leads at series A/B startups. I have a list of 200. I want one cold email that I can lightly personalise per recipient. The product is $12/user/month. We have 4 reference customers (Linear, Vercel, Render, Modal). The differentiator is that we integrate with GitHub PRs and Slack, so the standup writes itself from activity rather than asking each engineer to fill out a form. Voice: founder, plain, direct. No marketing.

Inputs extracted:

- One thing: get the engineering lead to reply.
- Reader: VP Eng or Head of Engineering at a series A or B startup. Has 10 to 60 engineers. Has tried async standup tools before and abandoned them because no one filled out the forms.
- Belief shift: from "async standup tools require my team to do extra work" to "this one runs off their actual work."
- Promise: the standup writes itself from PRs and Slack activity.
- Proof: Linear, Vercel, Render, Modal as references. Specific GitHub + Slack integration.
- Format: cold email, 75 to 130 words.
- Voice: Voice 8 (founder narrator) blended with Voice 1 (dry confident). First person, specific, no sell.
- CTA: a reply or a 15 minute call.

---

## The output

### Idea
The async standup that does not ask your team to write the standup.

### Final copy

**Subject (variant A, the production subject)**
> Async standups your team will actually do

**Subject (variant B, for recipients who have written publicly about meetings)**
> About the post-standup meeting your team still has

**Subject (variant C, for recipients at companies on our existing customer roster)**
> Pulse, Linear, and your team

**Body**
> Hi Maya,
>
> I saw your January post about cutting your standup from 25 minutes to 7. The reason I am writing is that even after the cut, half the engineering leads I talk to are still running a 7 minute standup that does not need to exist.
>
> I run Pulse. It is an async standup tool that writes the standup itself from GitHub PRs and Slack activity. Your team does not fill out a form. They keep working. The standup arrives in Slack at 10am and the lead reads it in 90 seconds.
>
> Linear, Vercel, Render, and Modal run on Pulse. Engineers stopped filling out forms. Leads stopped chasing them.
>
> Worth a 15 minute call next week to see if it fits?
>
> Tato
> pulse.tools/maya (a 90 second video walkthrough)

### Alternates

1. **Lead with the customer logo first.** "Linear, Vercel, Render, and Modal run their engineering standup on something I built. The reason it works..." Tradeoff: leads with social proof but loses the personalisation that earns the read.
2. **Open with a question.** "Does your team still write a daily standup post?" Tradeoff: lighter open, weaker because the personalisation hook (the January post) is the strongest line in this email.
3. **Drop the link entirely.** Some founders close cold emails with no link at all and rely on the reply. Tradeoff: the link reduces friction for the curious; dropping it pushes the reader into a slower channel.

### Notes

Word count: 129 words measured, including greeting, sign-off, and link line. Inside the 130-word cold email upper bound, with no room to spare; the next edit must cut, not add.

Framework doing the work: the five-line cold email structure from `formats.md`. Line one names a specific reason for writing this person (Maya's January post). Line two reframes Pulse in terms of her problem (the standup that does not need to exist). Line three is the proof (four named customers). Line four is the ask (a 15 minute call, the smallest defensible request). Sign off is the founder's name plus one link.

Voice craft moves applied:
- First person, named ("I run Pulse"). The founder is the brand. Voice 8.
- Plain verbs ("writes," "arrives," "reads," "stopped"). No "transforms," "empowers," "unlocks."
- Specifics: 25 to 7 minutes, 90 seconds, 10am, four named customers.
- The dry confidence of Voice 1 carries the last paragraph: "Engineers stopped filling out forms. Leads stopped chasing them." Two short sentences, parallel, no adjective.

Self-edit pass: read aloud at conversation pace. The 122 words read in roughly 30 seconds. The opener references a specific public post (the user provided the reference; in production, the personalisation field would be confirmed per recipient). No banned vocabulary. No em dashes. The CTA is a specific small ask ("15 minute call next week") not a vague "worth chatting?"

---

## Postmortem: what the skill did

The briefing pass identified the real differentiator: the standup writes itself. Most async standup tools sell the meeting reduction. Pulse sells the absence of the form. That distinction is the entire email.

The personalisation field (the January post) is the line that converts the email from cold to warm. Without it, the email is a template. With it, it is a letter. The skill flagged the personalisation as the most important variable and gave the user one production subject plus two situational alternates so they can pick the strongest variant per recipient.

The proof line (Linear, Vercel, Render, Modal) is run as a single sentence and immediately followed by the consequence. The skill avoided the trap of running a "trusted by" logo wall in cold email copy. Names plus consequence is stronger than names alone.

The CTA is one ask. The video walkthrough is a low-friction alternative for recipients who do not want to commit to a call yet. The skill picked "15 minute call" instead of "demo" because the reader has had bad demos before and the smaller, more specific ask earns more replies.

What to steal from this example: cold email is won at line one. If the personalisation field is real and specific, the rest of the email earns its read. If it is generic, no amount of polish on lines two through five recovers it. Write the personalisation field as a real human sentence, not as a mail-merge token.
