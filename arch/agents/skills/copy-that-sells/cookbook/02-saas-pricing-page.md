# Example 2: B2B SaaS pricing page

A worked example of the skill applied to a pricing page for a fictional B2B SaaS product. The product is called "Ledgerline," a reconciliation tool for finance teams.

---

## The brief from the user

> We need a pricing page for Ledgerline. We have three plans: Free, Growth, and Scale. Free is for 1 user, 50 transactions a month. Growth is $49 a month, 5 users, 2,000 transactions. Scale is $199 a month, unlimited users, 25,000 transactions. The buyer is a finance team lead at a 50 to 500 person company. They are evaluating us against three competitors who charge more and offer less. The current pricing page is generic and converts at 1.2 percent. Voice: technical, plain, confident. No fluff.

Inputs extracted:

- One thing: get a 50-to-500 person finance team to start a trial.
- Reader: finance team lead, mid-market. Has used at least one competing tool. Cares about reconciliation speed, audit trail, and integration with their existing stack.
- Belief shift: from "all reconciliation tools are the same" to "Ledgerline is faster and cheaper than the alternatives I have already tried."
- Promise: "Reconcile a month of transactions in 9 minutes instead of two days."
- Proof: per-plan transaction limits, customer logos, a side-by-side comparison.
- Format: pricing page, web. 30 to 80 words per plan plus FAQ.
- Voice: Voice 9 (technical precise) blended with Voice 1 (dry confident).
- CTA: Start free.

---

## The output

### Idea
The cheapest reconciliation tool that audits as cleanly as the expensive ones.

### Final copy

**Page headline**
> Pricing

**Plan grid intro (one line above the grid)**
> Same audit trail at every plan. The difference is transaction volume.

**Plan: Free**
> For solo operators and finance students.
> $0 forever.
> - 1 user
> - 50 transactions a month
> - Full audit log
> - 7 day support response
>
> CTA: Start free

**Plan: Growth** (most popular)
> For finance teams of 5 to 50 people closing the month on schedule.
> $49 a month.
> - 5 users
> - 2,000 transactions a month
> - SOC 2 audit trail
> - Same day support
> - 4-hour onboarding session
>
> CTA: Start the trial

**Plan: Scale**
> For finance teams that close in 9 minutes regardless of volume.
> $199 a month.
> - Unlimited users
> - 25,000 transactions a month
> - SOC 2, ISO 27001, SAML SSO
> - 1 hour support response
> - Custom integration with your ERP
>
> CTA: Talk to sales

**FAQ (6 questions)**

> **What happens after the 14 day trial?**
> Your account moves to Free unless you pick a plan. No card on file, no auto charge.
>
> **Can I switch plans later?**
> Yes. Upgrades take effect immediately. Downgrades take effect at the start of the next billing cycle.
>
> **Do you have a refund policy?**
> Yes. 60 days. If Ledgerline does not save you 8 hours in your first month of use, we refund the difference, no questions.
>
> **How does Ledgerline compare to (competitor)?**
> Two thirds the price. Same SOC 2. Faster reconcile by a factor of 4 on our public benchmark.
>
> **Will my data be safe?**
> SOC 2 Type II. Read replicas in two regions. We do not train on your data.
>
> **Can I see the audit trail before I buy?**
> Yes. Click here for a sample audit log from a real (anonymised) customer close.

**Risk reversal under the grid**
> 14 day trial. No card up front. 60 day refund.

### Alternates

1. **Two plans instead of three.** Some pricing pages convert better at two. Test "Growth" and "Scale" only with Free as a separate "free forever" callout.
2. **Annual toggle by default.** Show monthly + annual prices side by side; default to annual to lower the perceived price.
3. **"Pay what fits" callout under Scale** for non-profits and academic users.
4. **Comparison table replacing the plan grid.** Risk: pricing pages with comparison tables convert worse than plan grids for first time visitors but better for return visitors. Use only if traffic is mostly returning.

### Notes

Word count: 220 words across the three plans, 145 in the FAQ, 12 in risk reversal. Inside the format limit.

Framework doing the work: AIDA at the page level. Attention is the headline "Pricing" + intro line that names the differentiator. Interest is the plan grid where the differentiator (transaction volume + same audit trail) does the work. Desire is the proof of refund and benchmark. Action is three CTAs of different friction (Start free, Start the trial, Talk to sales).

Bly Reason-why was used in each plan description: not just "5 users" but "for finance teams of 5 to 50 people closing the month on schedule." The reason ("closing the month on schedule") teaches the buyer who the plan is for, not just what is in it.

Voice 9 craft moves applied:
- No marketing verbs (no "transform," "unlock," "empower"). Used "reconcile," "audit," "close" instead.
- Real specifics (60 day refund, 9 minutes, factor of 4 benchmark, 7 day vs same day vs 1 hour support).
- The voice holds across the FAQ. The FAQ is the test of whether voice survives at low information density.

Self-edit pass: banned words check clean. CTA verbs are specific (Start free, Start the trial, Talk to sales). No em dashes. No bulleted bold titles inside paragraphs. The plan bullets are genuinely list-like content, not bolded-title prose.

---

## Postmortem: what the skill did

The briefing pass surfaced the real differentiator: same audit trail at every plan. The user had not stated it that way. The skill named it, the user confirmed, and that became the line above the plan grid. Without that line, the grid reads as three feature lists and the buyer cannot tell which plan they belong in.

The reason-why craft moves under each plan turned the bullets from a feature list into a positioning tool. "For finance teams of 5 to 50 people closing the month on schedule" tells a 30-person finance lead exactly where they are in the grid.

The FAQ does triple duty: it handles objections, it shows pricing transparency (no auto charge, 60 day refund), and it positions against competitors without naming them in a way that creates legal exposure. The benchmark claim ("faster reconcile by a factor of 4") is flagged in Notes as requiring verification before publishing.

What to steal from this example: pricing pages convert when the plan grid teaches the buyer where they belong, not just what they get. Reason-why copy inside plan descriptions is the lever. The intro line above the grid is the single most important sentence on a pricing page.
