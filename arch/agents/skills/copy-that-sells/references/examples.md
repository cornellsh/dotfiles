# Examples: A Working Library of Copy That Sold

152 pieces of print, OOH, social, and direct-response work organised by technique. Mix of timeless classics that defined the craft, modern always-on brand voice, and award winners from roughly 2001 to 2025 at Cannes Lions, Clio Awards, D&AD, One Show, El Ojo de Iberoamérica, and El Sol. Use as a quick-fire reference when looking for a way in.

Attribution conventions: brand, year (approximate where uncertain), agency, plus the verbatim headline where the line is famous enough to quote confidently. Where the work is more visual than verbal, the description does the work. Award labels (Cannes, Clio, D&AD, One Show) are noted where the work is a known winner.

Editorial rule: no example enters this file without a verified attribution (festival archive, trade press, or the brand itself). An entry that cannot be verified gets replaced, not defended. This file is the skill's proof library; a fabricated proof here poisons every output that cites it.

Live galleries worth checking for current craft:

- clios.com/winners-gallery. Searchable Clio archive.

## How to use this file

Scan the section headings to find the technique your brief needs. Each example is a proof-of-existence: it shows that this move works. Steal the structure, not the words.

## 1. Compression: entire idea in three to six words

- **Nike** "Just do it." (Wieden+Kennedy, 1988). Three words. Names a universal hesitation and dismisses it. Works on a billboard, a sneaker box, a Twitter bio.
- **Stella Artois** "Reassuringly expensive." (Lowe Howard-Spink, 1980s). Two words turn the price objection into the proof point.
- **Carlsberg** "Probably the best lager in the world." (Saatchi & Saatchi, 1973). The qualifier "probably" lands the joke and licenses the boast.
- **Avis** "We're number two. We try harder." (DDB, 1962, Paula Green). Self-deprecation as positioning. Built the company.
- **Apple** "Think different." (TBWA\Chiat\Day, 1997, Lee Clow / Craig Tanimoto). Two words and a manifesto. Worked across every medium for a decade.
- **BMW** "The Ultimate Driving Machine." (Ammirati Puris, 1975). Four words that defined the brand for fifty years.
- **De Beers** "A diamond is forever." (NW Ayer, 1947, Frances Gerety). One sentence that built a category and kept it.
- **FedEx** "When it absolutely, positively has to be there overnight." (Ally & Gargano, 1978). Functional, specific, complete.
- **L'Oreal** "Because you're worth it." (McCann, 1973, Ilon Specht). The justification embedded in the line.
- **M&Ms** "Melts in your mouth, not in your hand." (Bates, 1954). Comparison built into the line.
- **Ronseal** "Does exactly what it says on the tin." (HHCL, 1994). The product-name promise as the brand promise.
- **MasterCard** "There are some things money can't buy. For everything else, there's MasterCard." (McCann, 1997). The category reframe, in two clauses.

## 2. Headline + visual completion: neither works alone

- **Volkswagen** "Lemon." (DDB, 1960, Julian Koenig / Helmut Krone). Perfect-looking car. The headline says it is a reject. The body explains the standard.
- **Volkswagen** "Think Small." (DDB, 1959). Tiny car on a vast white page. The image is the proof of the headline.
- **Economist** "I never read The Economist. — Management trainee. Aged 42." (AMV BBDO, 1980s onward, David Abbott). The page is just yellow and the line. The reader supplies the meaning.
- **The Independent** "It is. Are you?" (Saatchi & Saatchi, 1986). The newspaper's name completes the line.
- **Penguin Books** "It will keep your mouth shut." (running campaign, various agencies). Image of a book; the line reframes reading as defence against being asked anything.
- **British Airways** "Magic of Flying." (OgilvyOne London, 2013, Cannes Direct Grand Prix 2014 and a Clio Grand Prix). Digital billboards in London where a child stood up and pointed at real BA planes passing overhead, naming the live flight number and destination. The headline was the sky.
- **British Airways** "A British Original." (Uncommon Creative Studio, 2023, Cannes Outdoor Grand Prix 2023). Over 500 print, digital, and OOH executions that played the real, human reasons people fly ("To say sorry in person") against the flat "Business or Leisure" tick-box on the booking form. The form was the foil; the reason was the ad. The follow-up "Windows" series ran customers' faces at the aircraft window with almost no copy at all.
- **Volkswagen** "King Arthur." (DDB classic series). The visual carries the story; the line is the punctuation.

## 3. Self-deprecation: truth against the brand

- **Avis** "We try harder." See Section 1. The original.
- **VW Beetle** body copy after Lemon: confessing the rejection rate as proof of quality control. The brand admits something embarrassing to prove the larger virtue.
- **Skoda** "It's a Skoda. Honest." (Fallon London, 2000s). Reclaiming the joke against the brand.
- **Spirit Airlines / Ryanair** "Less legroom. Lower fares." (various, ongoing). The reframe of a weakness as a benefit.
- **KFC** "FCK." (Mother London, 2018). Full-page newspaper apology after the chicken-supply crisis. The bucket logo, three letters, plain copy below. The most-praised apology ad in modern history because the brand told the truth on itself.
- **Domino's** "Our pizza was bad. We fixed it." (CP+B, 2009). The "Pizza Turnaround" campaign that put customer complaints on Times Square billboards. Doubled sales over four years.
- **Patagonia** "Don't Buy This Jacket." (in-house, NYT, Black Friday 2011). The brand argued against itself to sell its philosophy. Sold more jackets than ever.

## 4. Reframe: redefining the category

- **7Up** "The Uncola." (J. Walter Thompson, 1968). The category is everything except us.
- **Apple** "1984." (Chiat/Day, 1984, Lee Clow / Steve Hayden). Personal computing as rebellion against the corporate norm.
- **Dove** "Real Beauty." (Ogilvy, 2004 onward). The category is "beauty advertising," reframed as a betrayal the reader is invited to leave behind.
- **MINI** "Let's motor." (Crispin Porter + Bogusky, 2002). Driving as something the rest of the industry has forgotten how to talk about.
- **Innocent** "Tastes good. Does good." (in-house, 2000s). Smoothies as a kindness rather than a beverage.
- **Cravendale Milk** "Milk matters." (Wieden+Kennedy London, 2000s). Milk as an active ingredient rather than a commodity.
- **Lurpak** "Good food deserves Lurpak." (Wieden+Kennedy London, 2000s). Butter as the reward at the end of cooking.
- **Old Spice** "The man your man could smell like." (Wieden+Kennedy, 2010). Body wash for men, sold to the women buying it. Reframed the buyer.

## 5. Specificity: proof inside the line

- **Rolls-Royce** "At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock." (Ogilvy & Mather, 1958, David Ogilvy). The single most-imitated headline in advertising. The number is the proof. The detail is the proof.
- **Volvo** "Drive it like you hate it." (Scali McCabe Sloves, 1970s). Body listed the welds, the cage, the test data.
- **Hathaway** "The man in the Hathaway shirt." (Ogilvy, 1951). The model wore an eyepatch. The detail was the brand.
- **Volvo** "The Volvo 240 is the most carefully tested car you can buy." Body copy detailed each test. Specificity as the entire argument.
- **Penny** "Price Packs." (Serviceplan NEO Munich, 2024, Cannes Print & Publishing Grand Prix 2025). Printed the fixed retail price directly on the packaging of five staple products. The product was the proof of the promise: this is the price, it cannot change.
- **Tropicana** "Brighter Mornings for Brighter Days." (DDB, 2010, Cannes Outdoor Grand Prix 2010). Built a giant artificial sun in Inuvik during the Arctic dark season. The demonstration was the headline.
- **The Zimbabwean Newspaper** "Trillion Dollar Campaign." (TBWA\Hunt\Lascaris, 2009, Grand Clio + Cannes Outdoor Grand Prix 2009). Printed posters directly onto Zimbabwean trillion-dollar notes, then worthless from hyperinflation. The substrate was the headline. The currency became the medium became the message.
- **Mexico Tourism Board** various. Specific village names, specific dishes, specific roads, in long copy that made the country a list of particulars.

## 6. Question: the reader answers and is invested

- **Maxwell Sackheim** "Do you make these mistakes in English?" (1919, for the Sherwin Cody School of English). Among the most-tested headlines of the early twentieth century; it ran almost unchanged for four decades. Often misattributed to Caples, who analysed it but did not write it.
- **Clairol** "Does she... or doesn't she?" (Foote, Cone & Belding, 1956, Shirley Polykoff). Sold hair colour by asking the only question that mattered.
- **Listerine** "Often a bridesmaid but never a bride." (Lambert Pharmacal, 1925, Milton Feasley). Question in disguise. The reader fills in the cause.
- **Pepsi** "Is Pepsi OK?" (Goodby Silverstein & Partners, Super Bowl 2019, "More Than OK"). Reclaiming the dismissive restaurant line.
- **Anchor Butter** "How do you do yours?" (BMP, 1990s). The personal question to the reader.

## 7. Provocation / challenge: disturb the reader

- **The Economist** "Why should I do anything to change my image? I don't have one." (AMV BBDO, David Abbott). The reader is dared to recognise themselves in the line.
- **Greenpeace** dozens of poster campaigns whose lines confront the reader with the cost of inaction. The format: a hard image, a single sentence that says what you already half-knew.
- **Sandy Hook Promise** "Evan." (BBDO New York, 2016). Film, not print, but the principle: lure the viewer into one story, reveal the other story underneath.
- **Mexico City Police** various 2010s OOH that used the public to confront the public. The dare is the proof of moral seriousness.
- **WaterAid** various charity work that confronts the reader with what the absence of clean water costs. The line is small. The image is small. The agitation is in the gap between what you read and what you can do about it.

## 8. Long-copy seduction: the ad is an essay

- **Patek Philippe** "You never actually own a Patek Philippe. You merely look after it for the next generation." (Leagas Delaney, 1996, Tim Delaney). Long-copy series about fathers, sons, watches as inheritance. The line at the end says what the body has built up to.
- **The Economist** running long-copy series. Each ad a single argument. The reader who finishes is sold.
- **The Macallan** whisky long-copy series. The slow, specific reveal of the distillery, the casks, the maker. Length as evidence of craft.
- **Saturn (the car brand)** Hal Riney long-copy ads in the 1990s. Folksy, slow, intimate. The opposite of car-ad-bombast. The voice was the entire product.
- **Apple** "Here's to the crazy ones." (TBWA\Chiat\Day, 1997). Manifesto-length copy delivered both as TV voice-over and as print. The headline is famous; the body is the reason it sticks.
- **Volkswagen** "Lemon" body copy and the entire DDB long-copy era. The argument is the ad.
- **John Caples for U.S. School of Music** "They laughed when I sat down at the piano. But when I started to play!" (1926). The most imitated story-lead ad in direct response. The headline opens a narrative the body copy finishes; the reader stays for the revenge.
- **Mary Wells / Wells Rich Greene for Alka-Seltzer** "I can't believe I ate the whole thing." (1972). Long-format radio and TV; the line works because the entire surrounding monologue earns it.

## 9. Reason-why: claims supported in line

- **Volkswagen** "Why won't your wife let you drive it?" (DDB, 1960s). The body listed the reasons. The reasons did the selling.
- **Volvo** various reason-why ads listing safety features. The list was the case.
- **American Express** "Don't leave home without it." Body listed the reasons. The line stuck because the reasons were specific.
- **Hovis** "It's as good for you today as it's always been." Body copy described the milling, the wheat, the bakeries. The claim earned its weight.
- **The Times** various 2010s subscription ads built around "the seven things in tomorrow's paper" reason-why structures.

## 10. Object-as-headline: the product is the ad

- **Penny** "Price Packs." See Section 5. The package itself was the line.
- **The Zimbabwean Newspaper** "Trillion Dollar Campaign." See Section 5. The currency was the canvas.
- **Heineken** "Shutter Ads." (Publicis Italy, 2020, Cannes Outdoor Grand Prix 2021). Bought OOH media but redirected the spend to closed-bar shutters during Covid. The shutters became the headline.
- **The North Face** "Climbing Walls." (Leo Burnett Korea, 2010s). Posters in supermarkets and pharmacies repositioned product against the building, so the visual context completed the line.
- **IKEA** "Pee Ad." (Akestam Holst, 2018). A magazine ad that doubled as a pregnancy test. The product (a discounted crib) revealed when peed on. The ad was the test was the offer.
- **WWF** Surgical Mask Tiger. (TBWA\Singapore, 2020). Mask-shaped poster with a tiger illustration revealing the half of the face hidden by the mask. The object was the message.
- **Vodafone** "Underage Ads." (Ogilvy Czech Republic, 2019). Roadside billboards that displayed messages only at child-eye-level, the height a parent driving cannot see. The placement was the headline.
- **Havaianas** "Nest." (AlmapBBDO Sao Paulo, late 2000s, multiple Clio wins). Print series for the flip-flop brand showing impossible scenes built around the sandal. The product as recurring set piece across executions.

## 11. Demonstration in print: proof inside the page

- **Maxell** Blown Away Guy. (Scali McCabe Sloves, 1980; photographed by Steve Steigman). The blast from the speaker did the convincing.
- **Schwartz Spices** "Smell" execution. Magazine inserts that smelled of the spice. The page was the proof.
- **Volkswagen** "Snowplough." (DDB, 1963). Famous film, but the print version: an image of a snowplough and the line "Have you ever wondered how the man who drives the snowplough drives to the snowplough?" The reader has to think. The thinking is the demonstration.
- **Burger King** "Whopper Detour." (FCB New York, 2018). App-based campaign that unlocked a Whopper for one cent if the user opened the app at a McDonald's. The location was the demonstration.
- **Dove** "Real Beauty Sketches." (Ogilvy Brazil, 2013). Forensic sketch artist drew women based on their own descriptions and then on a stranger's description. The two drawings were the demonstration.

## 12. Series and running campaigns: the brand becomes a voice

- **Economist** ongoing yellow-and-white poster series, 1980s onward. Hundreds of executions, one voice, one visual identity.
- **Absolut** "Absolut [insert]" 1980s onward. Bottle as the constant; the world around it as the variable.
- **The Guardian** "The whole picture" (2017). One newsprint campaign, multiple executions, one editorial argument.
- **John Lewis** Christmas adverts (Adam&eveDDB, 2007 onward). Annual ritual. Reader expectation as half the work.
- **Specsavers** "Should've gone to Specsavers." (Specsavers Creative, ongoing since 2003). One line. Hundreds of executions. The line writes the next ad.
- **Mailchimp** brand campaign of misheard names ("MailKimp," "FailChips") (Droga5, 2017). A series that played the same joke at different speeds.

## 13. Public-service tonality: moral seriousness done with restraint

- **Amnesty International** print campaigns by Indra Sinha and others. Long copy that lays out the case with the discipline of a court filing. The voice is grave. The argument is the entire ad.
- **Sandy Hook Promise** "Evan." (BBDO New York, 2016). See Section 7.
- **Dumb Ways to Die** (McCann Melbourne, 2012, Cannes Film Grand Prix 2013, multiple Clio wins). Public service work that earned attention by ignoring the conventions of public service work.
- **British Heart Foundation** "Til I Died." (Saatchi & Saatchi London, 2024, Gold Clio OOH). OOH campaign honouring football fans lost to sudden cardiac death by treating each as a numbered shirt that never came home. Public-service tonality made specific by names.
- **RSPCA** various long-copy print by David Abbott. Animals, specific names, specific stories, specific outcomes.
- **The Newspaper Society** "The power of print" various, 2010s. Print arguing for itself, with the discipline of the argument.

## 14. Dare / insult the reader: used sparingly

- **VW** "Lemon." See Section 2. The brand insults itself, but the implied subject is the reader: you, who buy other cars, are accepting cars we would reject.
- **The Economist** "I never read The Economist." See Section 2. The implied insult is to the reader who has not yet started.
- **Diesel** "For Successful Living." (1990s onward, Paradiset DDB). The slogan as ironic critique of the reader's aspirations.
- **Spirit Airlines** "We're not sorry." (ongoing). The brand owns its reputation and turns it.

## 15. Truth-against-the-category: naming what everyone else does

- **Honest Tea** various ads about ingredient labels. The brand reads its own label aloud and the category looks shifty by comparison.
- **Innocent** drinks. The label voice that became the brand. "100 percent pure fruit smoothies, nothing added, nothing else." The category's evasions were the implicit comparison.
- **Oatly** running typographic campaigns 2018 onward. The brand argues for itself in the voice of a person who has noticed the cynicism of the category.
- **Liquid Death** running campaigns 2019 onward. Reframed water in a category that had stopped trying.

## 16. Cultural and political moments: copy as the news

- **Saatchi & Saatchi for the Conservative Party** "Labour isn't working." (1978-79). A snaking unemployment queue and three words. No body copy; the argument lives entirely in the pun and the image. Helped win the 1979 election.
- **Nike** "Dream Crazy." (Wieden+Kennedy, 2018, Cannes Outdoor Grand Prix 2019, with Colin Kaepernick). Single image, single line: "Believe in something. Even if it means sacrificing everything." The line is risk reversal for the brand and the reader together.
- **Audi** various Super Bowl print accompaniments on equal pay. The line and the statistic together.
- **The Washington Post** "Democracy Dies in Darkness." (2017). Tagline as editorial stance.
- **FAZ (Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung)** "100 Brilliant Minds." (Scholz & Friends, Cannes Print honours 2024). Anniversary campaign featuring Holocaust survivor Margot Friedländer at 102. The line was the photograph.

## 17. Latin-American and Spanish-language work to study

Agencies across Latin America and Spain have been quietly responsible for some of the strongest print and OOH craft of the last twenty years, and some of the sharpest recent ideas full stop. Worth studying in the original language. Award shows have noticed; Cannes, Clio, El Ojo de Iberoamérica and El Sol all run heavy on these names.

- **AlmapBBDO (Sao Paulo)** Volkswagen Brazil, Havaianas, Billboard magazine. The Havaianas series (multiple Clio + Cannes Lions wins) sold the flip-flop as a recurring character in absurd scenes. Volkswagen Brazil's print has won Lions almost every year of the past two decades.
- **DDB Brasil and DM9DDB** multiple D&AD Pencils and Cannes Lions across decades. Strong on Volkswagen, Pepsi, public service work.
- **DAVID (Sao Paulo and Buenos Aires)** Burger King Brazil "Burn That Ad" (2019, Cannes Mobile Grand Prix; not print but the print extensions of the same idea ran in dailies). Heineken work.
- **Africa (Sao Paulo)** Heineken Brazil, Itau bank, strong long-copy tradition.
- **Ogilvy & Mather Mexico** Cerveza Indio "Indio Stars" and "Indio Diccionario" series, multiple Cannes and Clio honours.
- **Leo Burnett Mexico City** McDonald's Mexico, multiple Cannes Lions and Clios in print.
- **Sancho BBDO (Bogota)** strong long-copy print tradition for Colombian brands and public service work.
- **JWT Mexico / Wunderman Mexico / Mayo (Buenos Aires)** running output across two decades of Latin-American Cannes and Clio winners.

Recent Spanish-language campaigns to study (each verified against a festival or trade source):

- **Tulipán** "Teniendo sexo podemos reactivar la economía." (Zurda with Retina, Argentina; Gran Ojo PR, El Ojo de Iberoamérica 2024.) The condom brand built a discount platform with 20-plus brands on the premise that sex drives consumer spending. The line turns an Argentine economic-recovery slogan into a shareable double meaning.
- **J&B** "Orgullo de Pueblo." (El Ruso de Rocky, Spain; Gran Premio Campañas Integradas, El Sol 2023.) Took LGTBI Pride to small rural towns on a float and named the "sexilio," the exodus of the rural queer community to the cities. The pun on "orgullo" (Pride, and pride of one's village) only works in Spanish.
- **Quilmes** "Coincidencias." (draftLine, in-house, Argentina; Qatar 2022 World Cup; Gran Premio Film, El Sol 2023.) Listed the coincidences between Argentina's 1986 title and 2022 to feed the hope of winning; it went viral again after the final. Conversational copy that is rioplatense to the bone.
- **Tecate** "Golfo de México." (LePub Mexico City; Gold and Silver Lions, Cannes 2025, Mexico's most-awarded campaign that year.) After the gulf was officially renamed "Gulf of America," Tecate built a real, geolocated floating bar defending the name "Golfo de México," with a Tecate Light edition made from gulf salt. The verbal stand is the whole idea.
- **BBVA México** "¿Es Fraude?" (Terán\TBWA; Gran Ojo Eficacia, El Ojo 2024; Effies México 2024.) A five-episode game show that teaches viewers to tell real bank messages from fraudulent ones. The title, posed as a question, turns fraud prevention into entertainment.

Reading recommendation when writing in Spanish: study these agencies in the language they actually wrote in. The rhythm of Latin-American Spanish copy is different from translated English. Spanish allows the headline to land later in the sentence in a way English does not; the agencies above know how to use that.

## 18. Modern brand voice: always-on social, packaging, and the DTC script

These brands built their reputations not on one famous ad but on a voice held across thousands of small touches: a tweet, a reply, a billboard byline, a pack of toilet paper, a launch video. This is the modern counterweight to the print-classic sections above. The craft is tone held at scale and a willingness to sound like a person, not a brand. Most are made in-house, which is part of the lesson: a single sign-off and a consistent voice beat a committee.

- **Duolingo** the unhinged Duo the owl voice (in-house social team, originated by Zaria Parvez, roughly 2021 onward). A language app turned its mascot into a chaotic, self-aware character that entertains first and sells almost never. The craft is the reply copy and the persona never breaking.
- **Ryanair** the self-roasting airline voice (in-house, peak roughly 2021 to 2023). The budget airline owns its own reputation, mocks its own legroom, and roasts the people in its replies. Low-fi on purpose; the reactive caption is the whole craft.
- **Liquid Death** "Greatest Hates." (in-house, ongoing). The brand turns real hate comments into actual recorded songs. The voice (canned water sold like a death-metal act) is the product. Proof that tone can carry a commodity.
- **Oatly** "Wow, no cow." and the packaging voice. (in-house, the self-named "Department of Mind Control" under John Schoolcraft). The deliberately bad jingle and the rambling, anti-marketing long copy on the carton are the brand. The category's evasions are the implicit contrast.
- **Surreal** the fake-celebrity billboards. (in-house, UK, 2023 onward). "We're Dwayne Johnson's favourite cereal," in small type: "Dwayne is a bus driver from London." A challenger cereal beat a celebrity budget with a footnote. Pure copy as the mechanism.
- **Who Gives A Crap** the brand voice across pack, web, and email. (in-house, ongoing). Warm, toilet-humour-forward tone applied consistently to a boring sustainable product. The lesson is consistency across every touch, not one campaign.
- **Dollar Shave Club** "Our blades are f***ing great." (founder Michael Dubin, 2012). A deadpan founder monologue shot in a day for about $4,500 that launched a category. The script is the brand; the DTC launch-video archetype.
- **Spotify** "Thanks 2016, It's Been Weird." (in-house, 2016 to 2017). Anonymised listening data mined into open-letter billboards ("Dear person who made a playlist called..."). Elite data-into-copy craft and the ancestor of the annual Wrapped OOH voice.

## 19. Recent award benchmarks (2022 to 2025)

Recent Grand Prix and benchmark work so the library does not date itself to the print era. Each entry is a current proof of a technique already in this file, set in a contemporary format: compression, object-as-headline, reframe, self-deprecation, specificity. Every entry is sourced to a festival or trade record, and a large subset was spot-checked directly during the last expansion.

- **AnNahar** "The Election Edition." (Impact BBDO Dubai, Cannes Print & Publishing Grand Prix 2022.) When Lebanon's government delayed elections blaming a paper-and-ink shortage, the 88-year-old paper stopped printing, donated its paper and ink for ballots, and ran empty news racks. The first non-existent newspaper to win the Print Grand Prix. The blank page is the argument.
- **Coinbase** the bouncing QR code. (Accenture Song, Cannes Direct Grand Prix 2022.) A 60-second Super Bowl spot of nothing but a colour-changing QR code drifting across the screen. Anti-advertising compression that crashed the app from traffic. Section 1 (Compression) taken to its limit.
- **Heinz** "Draw Ketchup." (Rethink Toronto, Cannes Print Gold 2022; the wider "It Has To Be Heinz" platform won the Creative Effectiveness Grand Prix 2024.) Asked to "draw ketchup," people worldwide drew the Heinz bottle, proving the brand is the category. The instruction is the whole idea. Section 15 (truth against the category).
- **Vice** "The Unfiltered History Tour." (Dentsu Webchutney, multiple Cannes Grands Prix 2022.) An unofficial AR audio tour of the British Museum telling the looted-origin stories of disputed artefacts from the colonised nations' point of view. Script-led; the most awarded work of the festival that year.
- **CALM x ITV** "The Last Photo." (adam&eveDDB, Cannes Film Grand Prix 2023.) Smiling portraits, revealed to be the last photos of people before they died by suicide, dismantling the myth that suicidal people "look" depressed. Insight-and-reveal in the public-service tradition of section 13.
- **Stella Artois** "The Artois Probability." (GUT Buenos Aires, Cannes Creative Data Grand Prix 2023, plus Print and Outdoor Gold.) An algorithm tagged classic paintings with the percentage probability that the figures are drinking Stella, by glass shape, liquid colour, date and place. Brueghel's Peasant Wedding: "78% probability of Stella Artois." Heritage proved by data, with the caption as the joke. Specificity (section 5).
- **Dove** "#TurnYourBack." (Ogilvy UK and DAVID Madrid, Cannes Media Grand Prix 2023.) Launched within 72 hours of TikTok's "Bold Glamour" filter, it asked women to turn their backs on the filter. The "Real Beauty" platform extended into a single line. Reframe (section 4).
- **Coca-Cola** "Recycle Me." (Ogilvy New York / WPP Open X, Cannes Print & Publishing Grand Prix 2024.) The world's most recognisable logo redrawn as it looks on a crushed can, with the two-word line lifted from the side of the can. Object-as-headline (section 10) and compression at once.
- **Specsavers** "The Misheard Version." (Golin London, Cannes Audio & Radio Grand Prix 2024 and PR Grand Prix 2024.) Rick Astley re-recorded "Never Gonna Give You Up" with the lyrics people mishear ("Never gonna run around with dessert spoons"), selling hearing tests. The wordplay is the product demo.
- **Orange** "WoMen's Football." (Marcel Paris, Cannes Film Grand Prix 2024.) A reel of dazzling goals "by the French men's team," revealed mid-film to be the women's team with swapped faces. The reveal weaponises the viewer's own bias.
- **CeraVe** "Michael CeraVe." (Ogilvy, Cannes Social & Influencer Grand Prix 2024, plus the Super Clio.) A four-week conspiracy that actor Michael Cera secretly invented CeraVe, built entirely on the coincidence in the name. The pun is the campaign.
- **IKEA** "Proudly Second Best." (DAVID Madrid and INGO Hamburg, Cannes Gold 2023.) The hero children's products (cot, high chair, stool) sit empty in the background because the child prefers a parent's arms. The brand calls itself the second-best option. A Spanish agency's idea, English copy.
- **Dramamine** "The Last Barf Bag." (FCB Chicago, Cannes Health & Wellness Grand Prix 2024.) A mini-documentary mourning the disappearing airline sickness bag, framing its decline as proof the anti-nausea brand works. Wry, idea-first storytelling around an unglamorous product.
- **KitKat** "Phone Break." (VML Prague, Cannes Outdoor Grand Prix 2024.) OOH built so passersby could physically snap a "break," stretching "Have a break, have a KitKat" into the chaos of public transport. A sixty-year-old tagline made into an object.
- **L'Oréal Paris** "The Final Copy of Ilon Specht." (IPG / McCann, dir. Ben Proudfoot, Cannes Film Grand Prix 2025.) A 17-minute documentary about Ilon Specht, the McCann copywriter who wrote "Because I'm Worth It." A film about a piece of copy and the woman behind four of advertising's most famous words. Required viewing for this skill.
- **Channel 4** "Considering What?" (4Creative, Cannes Film Grand Prix 2025.) A Paralympics film that confronts the condescending "good, considering their disability." The single word "considering" is the hook the whole film takes apart. Provocation (section 7).
- **AXA** "Three Words." (Publicis Conseil Paris, Cannes Direct Grand Prix and Dan Wieden Titanium Grand Prix 2025.) AXA added "and domestic violence" to a French home-insurance clause that already covered emergency relocation for fire and flood, turning a contract into a way out. The reframe of three words is the idea.

## 20. Tonal masterclasses worth studying

These are not technique categories but voices to study. When asked to write in a brand voice, read these and notice the lever each uses.

- **Howard Gossage** for Land Rover, Eagle Shirts, Beethoven Records. Conversational, witty, smart-friend voice. Address the reader as an equal.
- **Bill Bernbach** for Volkswagen, Avis. Dry honesty. Self-aware. The reader feels respected.
- **David Abbott** for Volvo, Economist, Sainsbury's. Plain language, devastating clarity, a single argument per ad.
- **Tim Delaney** for Patek Philippe, Adidas, Harvey Nichols. Quiet, intimate, slow-building. The big claim arrives after the small ones earn it.
- **Tony Cox** for various UK brands at CDP. Compression. The poster discipline.
- **Indra Sinha** for Amnesty, Metropolitan Police. Long-form moral argument. The voice of someone who has thought longer than the reader.
- **Neil French** for XO Beer, Kaminomoto, Singapore brands. Confident, almost rude, fully in command of the page.
- **Lee Clow / Steve Hayden** for Apple. Manifesto voice. The reader as participant in a movement.
- **Hal Riney** for Saturn, Bartles & Jaymes. Folksy. The voice of the small town. Slow, specific, intimate.
- **John Webster** for Cadbury's Smash, Honey Monster, Hofmeister Bear. Humour as the carrier. Character as the argument.

## How to use these in a session

When stuck on a brief:

1. Identify the technique your idea wants to use.
2. Open this file and read the matching section.
3. Read three or four examples in that section out loud.
4. Write your headline. It will be sharper.

When unsure which technique fits:

1. Re-read Section 1 (Compression) and Section 5 (Specificity). These two together fix most weak copy.
2. If you have a story to tell, study Section 8 (Long-copy seduction).
3. If you have a product that has not changed, study Section 4 (Reframe).
4. If the category is dishonest, study Section 3 (Self-deprecation) and Section 15 (Truth-against-the-category).

When showing the user alternates, draw from at least two different sections so the alternates actually differ.
