# Formats: Blueprints for the Twelve Copy Formats That Sell

The principles in `craft.md` and `frameworks.md` apply to every format. But each format has its own ceiling: a billboard is six words because the reader is moving, not because billboards are aesthetic. A pricing page section is 40 words because that is what the eye scans before clicking. This file lists the working blueprints. Word counts are upper bounds for the format, not targets to fill. Always compress below.

## Email subject line

**Word count.** 4 to 9 words. Mobile clients cut after roughly 40 characters in iOS Mail, 60 in Gmail web. Write the working part of the subject inside the first 35 characters.

**Job.** Earn the open. That is all. The subject line is not the email. It is the promise of the email.

**Strongest categories.**

- Curiosity question. "Why did our open rate drop in March?"
- Personal-sounding. "Quick favour" or "About yesterday."
- Specific number. "312 founders read this last week."
- Pattern interrupt. "I was wrong about cold email."

**Avoid.**

- Caps lock.
- Multiple exclamation marks.
- "Newsletter," "update," "weekly digest." These are filing labels, not opens.
- "RE:" or "FWD:" fakes. Burn trust on first read.
- Emoji unless the brand uses emoji consistently in voice.

**Before.** "Important update about our Q2 product launch and roadmap"
**After.** "We shipped the thing you asked for in February."

The after is shorter, specific, and references the reader's history with the brand.

**The preheader.** The 40 to 90 characters of preview text after the subject line in the inbox. It is the second half of the open decision and most senders waste it on "View in browser." Write it as the subject line's next thought, not its repeat. Subject: "We shipped the thing you asked for in February." Preheader: "Bulk export, plus the two filters nobody asked for but everyone uses." If the email template does not expose a preheader field, the first line of body copy becomes the preheader; write it knowing that.

**Self test.** If you swapped your brand name into a competitor's subject line, would it still make sense? If yes, the line is not unique enough. Read subject and preheader together as one two-line ad; that is how the inbox shows them.

## Cold email

**Word count.** 75 to 130 words total. Shorter is better. Anything over 200 words for a cold email does not get read.

**Structure.**

1. **Line one.** A specific reason you are writing this person, not someone else. Name a piece of work they did, a metric they shared, a moment they had publicly. If you cannot name the reason in one line, you do not have one.
2. **Line two.** The reframe of your offer in terms of their problem.
3. **Line three.** Proof, in one line. A number, a logo, a result.
4. **Line four.** The ask. One specific small request. A 15 minute call. A reply. A look at a one paragraph case study.
5. **Sign off.** Name. One link. That is it.

**Avoid.**

- "I hope this finds you well."
- "I came across your profile."
- "Just wanted to reach out."
- Any opening that does not name a specific reason for writing this person.
- Multiple links or attachments. One.
- A second CTA. One ask.

**Working example.**

> Subject: Your Stripe payout dashboard
>
> Hi Sara,
> The way you talk about reconciliation in your last podcast matched something we hear from every CFO we onboard.
> We built a layer on top of Stripe that closes the reconciliation gap in 9 minutes a month instead of two days.
> Mercado Libre, Brex, and Notion run on it.
> Worth a 15 minute call next week?
> Tato

**Self test.** Would the recipient know within five seconds why you are writing them and not someone else? If not, the personalisation is not real.

## Lifecycle and marketing email

**Word count.** 90 to 350 words depending on type. Onboarding email 90 to 150. Newsletter 200 to 350. Re-engagement 100 to 200.

**Structure for a newsletter.**

1. Subject. See Email subject line.
2. Lead paragraph. 30 to 60 words. The hook of the issue. Drop the reader into the moment. No warm up.
3. Body. 150 to 250 words. One argument or one story. Not three.
4. CTA. One. Specific.
5. Sign off. Personal. Named writer.

**Structure for a transactional or lifecycle email.**

1. Subject. The thing that happened, plainly.
2. First line. What happened, restated, with the relevant specific.
3. What to do next. One action.
4. Sign off if relevant.

**Avoid.**

- Long banner image at the top with no copy underneath.
- Three columns of equal weight.
- Multiple CTAs of equal prominence.
- "Hi {first_name}," with no greeting recovery if the name is missing.

## Landing page hero

**Word count.** Hero headline 4 to 9 words. Sub headline 12 to 20 words. CTA button 2 to 4 words. Total above the fold 20 to 35 words.

**Structure.**

1. **Eyebrow.** Optional. 2 to 4 words. Category position. "Banking for startups." "Email for makers."
2. **Headline.** The promise. The 4 U's apply.
3. **Sub headline.** One sentence that earns the click. Names the audience, the outcome, or the differentiator the headline did not.
4. **CTA button.** Verb plus what they get.
5. **Trust strip.** Logos, a number, a testimonial line. One of the three. Not all.

**Test of a working hero.** Cover the rest of the page. If the hero alone tells the reader what the product is, who it is for, and why it matters, the hero is done.

**Avoid.**

- "Welcome to" anything.
- Hero copy that describes the product instead of selling the outcome.
- A headline that requires the sub headline to make sense. The headline should stand alone.
- A stock photo of people in a meeting room.

**Working example.**

> Eyebrow: Banking for startups
> Headline: Move money the way you write code.
> Sub: API-first banking for builders who would rather not call a bank.
> CTA: Open an account

## Pricing page

**Word count.** 30 to 80 words per plan. Plus 80 to 150 words for the FAQ section underneath.

**Structure.**

1. **Page headline.** Often unnecessary. "Pricing" suffices. Or skip and let the plan grid be the headline.
2. **Plans.** 3 typically. Each plan: name, one-line description, price, 4 to 7 bullets of what is included, CTA button.
3. **Plan names.** Concrete. "Free," "Pro," "Team," "Business." Avoid invented names like "Voyager," "Galactic," "Pioneer."
4. **Most popular** badge on the recommended plan.
5. **FAQ.** 4 to 8 questions. Real objections: "What happens after the trial?" "Can I switch plans?" "Is there a contract?" "Do you have a refund policy?"
6. **Risk reversal.** Money back, no card up front, cancel any time. One specific.

**Avoid.**

- "Contact us" pricing without giving a range. The buyer wants a number to take to their team.
- Plan descriptions that read identically. Each plan should have a clear "who is this for."
- Bullet lists of 15 items per plan. The reader does not read them. Pick the 5 that matter.

## Sales page (long form)

**Word count.** 1,200 to 4,000 words. Length follows the price and the complexity of the offer.

**Structure.** Follow `frameworks.md` "Long-copy architecture for sales pages." Eleven sections in order: hero promise, problem, why current solutions fail, introduce the offer, mechanism of action, proof, objection handling, risk reversal, specific offer, call to action, PS.

**Section word count guide.**

- Hero promise: 30 to 60 words including headline.
- Problem: 100 to 250 words.
- Why current solutions fail: 150 to 350 words.
- Introduce the offer: 80 to 200 words.
- Mechanism: 150 to 400 words.
- Proof: 300 to 800 words (testimonials, logos, screenshots, numbers).
- Objections: 200 to 500 words.
- Risk reversal: 50 to 100 words.
- Specific offer: 80 to 200 words.
- CTA: 30 to 60 words.
- PS: 30 to 80 words.

**Avoid.**

- Padding sections with "value." Specifics or cut.
- Skipping the objection section because it feels negative. The reader is already objecting; address it on page or lose them.
- A weak PS. The PS is the second most read part of a sales page.

## Product or feature page

**Word count.** 200 to 500 words. Lighter than sales page, heavier than landing page hero.

**Structure.**

1. Hero. Same shape as landing page hero. Promise of the feature.
2. Demo. Image, GIF, or video that shows the thing working. The visual is half the page.
3. Three to five sub sections, each with a small headline, a 30 to 60 word description, and a visual. Each sub section names one capability.
4. Proof. One or two testimonials specific to this feature.
5. Adjacent features. 2 to 3 links to related feature pages.
6. CTA.

**Test.** A reader who skims only the headlines should still understand what the feature does and what it gives them.

## Billboard and OOH

**Word count.** 1 to 7 words for the headline. Logo. Optional one-line sub if the format allows. No body copy. The image plus the line is the whole ad.

**Three-second test.** A driver passes the board at 60 km per hour. They have three seconds. Read the headline aloud at speaking pace. If it does not fit in three seconds with the meaning landing, it is too long.

**Structures that work.**

- One word plus visual. "Lemon" plus Volkswagen.
- Object as headline. The product on the board, used in a way that becomes the line.
- Self-deprecation in three words. "We try harder."
- Number plus product. "0,3 segundos."
- Location-aware line. "Bus stop two minutes from your office. Stop running, start riding."

**Avoid.**

- Multiple sentences.
- Phone numbers or URLs the reader cannot read at 60 km per hour.
- QR codes without a reason to scan.
- Copy that requires reading the small print at the bottom.

## Paid social ad

**Word count.** Primary text 40 to 90 words. Headline 5 to 10 words. Description line if used 20 to 30 words.

**Format-specific notes.**

- **Meta (Instagram, Facebook).** The image or video is half the ad. Copy is the other half. The first three lines of primary text are above the "see more" cut. Front-load the hook.
- **X (Twitter).** 280 characters total. The whole ad is one tweet. Treat as a billboard plus link.
- **TikTok.** The first second is the entire ad. The text overlay matters more than the description. Write the overlay as a headline.
- **LinkedIn.** Slightly more formal register. Specifics still win. Avoid the "I'm proud to announce" opener that the platform encourages.

**Structure for Meta.**

1. **Line one.** Hook. Stop the scroll. Pattern interrupt, question, or specific number.
2. **Line two.** Context. Who this is for, what the offer is.
3. **Line three to four.** Proof or detail.
4. **Line five.** CTA. Plain.

**Hook bank.** The first line is most of the ad. Fifteen structures that stop the scroll, grouped by mechanism. Fill them with the brief's specifics; the structure without a specific is just noise.

*Confession and cost:*
1. The mistake, owned. "We priced this wrong for two years."
2. The number that hurts. "Your weekly report costs you 104 hours a year."
3. The thing nobody says. "Most async standup tools die because nobody fills out the form."

*Pattern interrupt:*
4. The reversal of the expected claim. "Do not buy the bigger plan."
5. The wrong-category statement. "This is not a savings app. It is a rounding error with a bank account."
6. The unfinished sentence the image completes. "The last invoice you will ever build by hand looks like"

*Reader's mirror:*
7. The moment, named. "It is 9:40 on Tuesday and the report is still not done."
8. POV framing. "POV: the standup wrote itself while you reviewed PRs."
9. The objection, voiced first. "You think your team will not adopt another tool. Correct. Here is why this is not another tool."

*Receipts:*
10. Before and after with numbers. "Two days to reconcile. Now 9 minutes."
11. The named customer doing the thing. "How Linear runs standup with zero meetings."
12. The public test. "We sent 32 cold emails this week. 11 replies. The template is below."

*Tension and curiosity:*
13. The countdown or deadline that is real. "Price goes up Friday. This is the math for waiting."
14. The forbidden comparison. "Cheaper than the tool you are using, and it does the part you actually need."
15. The question only the right reader feels. "How many tabs does your monthly close take?"

Match the hook to the awareness level: mirrors and confessions for problem-aware readers, receipts and comparisons for product-aware, deadlines and offers for most aware.

**Avoid.**

- Hashtag stacks at the bottom.
- "Click the link in bio" when there is a real link button below.
- Stock photo overlaid with text. Either the image earns the ad or it does not.

## Paid search ad (responsive search ads)

**Format reality.** Google retired expanded text ads in 2022. The unit is now the responsive search ad: up to 15 headlines of 30 characters and 4 descriptions of 90 characters, which Google mixes and matches per auction. You are not writing one ad; you are writing an inventory of interchangeable parts.

**Word count.** Write 8 to 12 headlines (15 if the offer is rich) and all 4 descriptions. Every headline must make sense next to any other two, in any order.

**Headline inventory, by job.**

1. **Query echo (2 to 3 headlines).** The search query reflected back, refined. Search: "best CRM for small business." Headline: "CRM for Small Business Teams."
2. **Differentiator (2 to 3).** The benefit with a number. "Set Up in 9 Minutes."
3. **Proof (2 to 3).** Social proof or named result. "12,400 Teams Run on It."
4. **Risk reversal (1 to 2).** "Free for 14 Days. No Card."
5. **CTA (1 to 2).** Verb plus outcome. "Start Reconciling Today."

**Descriptions.** Each one a complete sentence that could close the ad alone: one expands the differentiator, one stacks proof, one handles the price objection, one is the offer plus CTA.

**Pinning.** Pin a headline to position 1 only when compliance or brand demands it; pinning reduces the combinations Google can test and usually lowers the Ad Strength rating. Accept the trade knowingly, not by default.

**Avoid.**

- Headlines that repeat the same first word; Google often shows three together and the repetition reads as a stutter.
- Generic verbs ("Discover," "Explore," "Learn"). Use specific verbs that imply the work being saved.
- Promotion language without the promotion present on the landing page.
- Writing 15 headlines by diluting 5 good ones. Inventory quality beats inventory volume; stop when the next headline would be filler.

## Push notification

**Word count.** Title 5 to 8 words. Body 12 to 25 words. Total under 35 words.

**Structure.**

1. **Title.** What happened or what is offered. Plain.
2. **Body.** One sentence that earns the tap. Specifics or stop.

**Categories that earn taps.**

- News specific to the user. "Your package is two stops away."
- A question only they can answer. "Three friends posted while you were away. See them now?"
- A reason to act today. "Your trial ends in 6 hours. Two clicks to keep your work."

**Avoid.**

- "Hey there!" or "Hi {first_name}!"
- "Don't miss out." Empty urgency.
- Re-engagement pushes that name no specific reason.

## App Store listing

**Word count.**

- **App name.** Up to 30 characters. Use the brand. Optional 2 to 3 word subtitle inside if there is room.
- **Subtitle (iOS).** 30 characters. The one-line promise.
- **Promotional text (iOS, 170 characters).** What is new now, or the one promise.
- **Description.** First 3 lines are above the "more" fold. Write the entire ad in those three lines. The rest of the description is for SEO and detail.
- **Screenshot captions.** 4 to 8 words each. The screenshot is the ad; the caption frames it.

**Working example (subtitle).**

> Banking that opens accounts in 9 minutes.

**Avoid.**

- Description that lists features in a wall of text. The reader does not scroll.
- Screenshot captions that describe the screenshot. The caption should add the promise the screenshot cannot.
- ASO keyword spam at the cost of readability.

## Manifesto

**Word count.** 80 to 400 words. The classic Apple "Here's to the crazy ones" is 165 words. That is the upper end of taut. The lower end is the Patek Philippe single line.

**Structure.**

1. **Open with a stance.** Not a description. A position. "We believe X." Or, stronger, "Most people think X. We think Y."
2. **Build the case.** Specifics, not abstractions. Why this matters now. What is at stake.
3. **Name the enemy.** Manifestos work because they name what they are against. The competitor, the category orthodoxy, the false promise, the easy path.
4. **The shift.** The future the manifesto wants. Concrete.
5. **The invitation.** Closing line. Quiet, not shouty. "Join us" is weak. "Build it with us" is stronger. The best manifestos end on an observation, not a button.

**Avoid.**

- Anaphora used as a crutch. "We believe X. We believe Y. We believe Z." Powerful once, exhausted by the third repetition.
- "Imagine a world where..." This opening kills the manifesto.
- Generic enemies. "Mediocrity" is not an enemy. "The 40-hour reporting cycle" is.

## Pre-roll video script

**Word count.** 6 second pre-roll: 12 to 18 words spoken. 15 second pre-roll: 30 to 45 words spoken. 30 second pre-roll: 60 to 80 words spoken.

**Structure for 6 seconds.**

One idea. One line of dialogue or one strong VO line. The product or brand in the frame. The CTA is implicit.

**Structure for 15 seconds.**

1. Hook (3 seconds). Pattern interrupt or specific stat.
2. Promise or demonstration (8 seconds).
3. Brand and CTA (4 seconds).

**Structure for 30 seconds.**

1. Hook (5 seconds).
2. Problem and demonstration (15 seconds).
3. Proof or testimonial (5 seconds).
4. Brand and CTA (5 seconds).

**Avoid.**

- Mention the brand only at the end. Skippers will hit skip before they see it. Plant the brand by second 4 of any pre-roll.
- Voice-over that paces faster than a human can naturally speak. Read the script aloud at performance pace before approving the word count.
- A CTA that requires the viewer to remember a URL. They will not.
