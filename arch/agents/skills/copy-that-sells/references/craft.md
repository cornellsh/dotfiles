# Craft: Lessons from The D&AD Copy Book

The D&AD Copy Book collects essays from many of the great copywriters of the last 60 years. Bernbach. Abbott. Cox. Sinha. French. Brignull. Delaney. Hayden. Webster. Holmes. Different brands, different decades, same craft. This document is a working distillation of what they teach, applied as instructions to follow when writing.

## The idea before the words

The most common mistake in copy is to start writing before the idea is sharp.

Bill Bernbach's principle: "An idea can turn to dust or magic, depending on the talent that rubs against it." The talent is wasted if the idea is mush.

David Abbott, who wrote some of the greatest Economist ads ever made, started every brief by writing down the proposition in plain English. A single sentence. If he could not write the proposition, he could not write the ad.

A working method:

1. State the proposition in one sentence. "This car is the safest you can buy." Boring. Right.
2. State the idea in one sentence. The idea is the angle on the proposition, the way you will make it feel new, the way you will make it stick. "The reason the safest car is also the dullest is that being dull is a side-effect of being safe."
3. The headline is one of several possible expressions of the idea. Generate three or four headlines for the chosen idea. Pick the strongest.

Skipping step 1 makes ads that say nothing. Skipping step 2 makes ads that say something but are forgettable. Skipping step 3 makes ads that have an idea but bury it in the wrong line.

The Volvo ad whose proposition is "Volvo is safe" had its idea in the observation that safety is the unsexy choice. The headline was something close to "Buy this car for the things you cannot see in the showroom." The body listed the welds, the steel, the cage. The ad sold the unsexy choice by naming it.

## The headline and the visual are one unit

In print and OOH, the headline and the visual together are the ad. Neither works alone.

The Volkswagen "Lemon" ad shows a perfect-looking Beetle. The headline says "Lemon." The body explains that the inspector found a blemished glove-compartment knob and rejected the car. The headline only works because the visual contradicts it. The visual only works because the headline contradicts it. The tension between the two is the idea.

The Avis ad showed a small piece of copy and a small Avis logo. The headline said "We're number two. We try harder." The visual was the absence of visual swagger. The art direction enacted the proposition.

Practical rules for copywriters working with art directors (or working alone):

1. Never write the headline before you know what the visual will be. The headline depends on it.
2. If the visual is doing the work, write less. A great image with a great line beats a great image with a paragraph.
3. If the headline says exactly what the visual shows, one of them is redundant. Make the headline say something the visual cannot.
4. The line and the image should together produce a single thought that neither produces alone. This is the test.

For homepage heroes, social ads, deck cover slides: same principle. Headline and image as a unit. If you can imagine swapping the image for any stock photo without losing meaning, the headline is doing all the work and the image is decoration. Decorative images do not sell.

## Compression

Tony Cox wrote that the discipline of poster copy is the discipline of compression. You have three seconds. You have six words. The reader is moving. The reader is bored. The reader is annoyed at being advertised to. Get out of their way.

How to compress:

- Cut every adjective.
- Cut every adverb.
- Cut every connective word ("therefore," "however," "moreover").
- Cut every word the reader can supply themselves.
- Cut the brand name if the logo is present.
- Cut the product name if the visual makes it obvious.
- Cut the explanation if the headline plus visual carries the idea.

Then count the words. Then cut another word.

The Economist ran a series of yellow-and-white posters with no images. The lines were the entire ad. "I never read The Economist. — Management trainee. Aged 42." Eleven words, including the age. Every word is doing work. The age is the punchline. The dash and the cadence give it the rhythm of a real quote.

This level of compression is the goal even when the format does not demand it. A homepage hero with the discipline of a billboard outperforms a homepage hero with the slackness of a brochure.

## Voice and rhythm

Copy is read with the inner voice. Rhythm matters more than grammar. Bly knew it. Cox knew it. Abbott knew it.

Three rhythmic moves that work nearly always:

1. **Short after long.** A sentence of fifteen or twenty words followed by a sentence of three. The short one lands.

2. **Parallel structure.** Two or three clauses with the same grammatical shape, ending differently. "It does not blink. It does not flinch. It just keeps working."

3. **The unexpected stop.** Set up an expectation, then end early. "We make a product that nobody asked for. And everybody bought."

Avoid:
- Stacked compound sentences.
- Sub-clauses that bury the verb.
- Long sentences with no pivot.
- Two consecutive sentences of the same length and shape.

A good test: count the syllables in each sentence. If they are all within two or three of each other, the rhythm is flat. Vary deliberately.

## Reading aloud

Every D&AD copywriter teaches this. Read it aloud.

If you stumble, the reader stumbles. If you run out of breath, the sentence is too long. If a word feels embarrassing to say, the reader hears it as embarrassing too. If you put on a stagey voice to deliver a line, the line is corporate, not human.

Read aloud after the first draft. Read aloud after every edit. Read aloud one last time before shipping.

This is the single most reliable test of whether copy is alive or dead.

## Truth and self-deprecation

The Avis line "We're number two. We try harder" is famous because it tells a truth against the brand. Self-deprecation, used surgically, creates more trust than any list of features.

The mechanism: when a brand admits a weakness, the reader is disarmed. The reader's default position is suspicion. Self-deprecation breaks the suspicion. Everything the brand says next is heard as more credible.

But self-deprecation has to be true. And it has to be small enough that the brand can recover from it within the same ad. "Our food is mediocre" is not useful self-deprecation. "We're a no-frills airline. So our seats don't recline. But our fares do". That works because it reframes the weakness as a benefit.

Other forms of truth-telling that build trust:

- Specifics rather than generalities ("at sixty miles an hour" not "very quietly")
- Naming the competitor by name and the comparison being fair
- Showing the demonstration on camera rather than describing it
- Pricing the product clearly rather than hiding it
- Acknowledging the doubt the reader is having ("I know what you are thinking. Two hundred pounds for a kettle. Read on.")

Trust compounds. Lies, even small ones, destroy what came before.

## The long-copy ad

The long-copy ad is the heart of the D&AD Copy Book. Tim Delaney's Patek Philippe ads. Indra Sinha's Amnesty work. David Abbott's Volvo. Tony Brignull's Ovaltine. These ads sometimes ran 700, 1,000, 1,500 words on a single page of a newspaper or magazine.

The bet of long copy: an interested reader will read every word, and a great long-copy ad converts that interest into action.

What makes long copy work:

1. **The headline earns the first paragraph.** It promises something the reader genuinely wants to know.

2. **The first paragraph earns the second.** No warm-up. Open at the most interesting point. Drop the reader into the middle of the argument.

3. **Each paragraph is a small story.** A claim, a piece of evidence, a turn. The reader is rewarded for staying.

4. **The argument has a shape.** A long-copy ad is structured like a closing argument in a trial. The reader is the jury. The structure is: here is the situation, here is what most people believe, here is what is actually true, here is the proof, here is what you should do about it.

5. **The voice is consistent.** A long-copy ad is a monologue. Pick a voice (dry, warm, urgent, intimate) and hold it from headline to sign-off.

6. **The close is small, not loud.** Long copy that builds beautifully and ends with a screaming CTA betrays itself. End the way you started. A quiet instruction. A small invitation. The work has already been done.

The Patek Philippe long-copy line "You never actually own a Patek Philippe. You merely look after it for the next generation" sits in long-copy ads about heritage, craft, and the generations that own the watch. The body builds a case. The line at the end is the conclusion the reader already feels.

## What to do when nothing is working

Every copywriter hits the wall. Tony Brignull's advice: when nothing is working, go back to the brief.

In practice:

1. **Read the brief out loud.** If it does not make sense to you, it will not make sense to the reader. Rewrite the brief in plain English before writing copy.

2. **Talk to a customer.** Find one real person who has bought the product. Ask them what they almost-bought instead, what nearly stopped them, what made them choose. The answers are the copy.

3. **Find the second-best idea.** If your best idea is not working in headlines, the idea may be wrong. Go back to your list of ideas. Pick the second one. Try.

4. **Steal a structure.** Look at a long-copy ad you admire. Map its structure onto your brief. Once the bones are in place, the muscles follow.

5. **Walk away.** Bernbach said the unconscious does more of the work than the conscious. Stop trying for an hour. Walk. Cook. Drive. The line arrives when you stop pushing.

6. **Write the ad you would never run.** Write the most over-the-top, most embarrassing, most extreme version. It will not be the ad. But it will tell you where the energy is. Then dial it back to a version you can run.

## A working list of principles to write by

From the collected D&AD essays, in plain language:

- Find the idea before the words.
- Pair every line with the image it lives next to.
- Compress until every word is necessary.
- Vary your sentence length.
- Read it aloud.
- Tell the truth, including against yourself when useful.
- Use specifics. "At sixty miles an hour" beats "very quietly."
- The reader is smart. Do not over-explain.
- Long copy works if the long copy is good.
- The close is small. The body has done the work.
- When stuck, go back to the brief and to a real customer.
