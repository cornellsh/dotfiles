# Voice Bank: Fifteen Voices to Pick From

Voice is the single largest lever in copy after the idea. The same proposition written in two different voices reads as two different products. This file is a working bank of fifteen voices (twelve from the English-language tradition, three native Spanish registers), each with sample lines and a recipe for when to use it. Use it during the briefing pass when the user has no voice guide, or during the diagnostic pass when the symptom is "it does not sound like us."

## How to use this file

Three modes:

1. **No voice guide.** Read the fifteen voices below. Pick the one closest to what the brand wants to be. Confirm with the user before writing: "I am writing this in a Voice 3 register. Tell me if you want a different lever."
2. **User has reference copy.** Read three to five samples from the user's existing material. Match the closest voice from this list and name it back to the user so they can confirm or correct. "Your existing copy reads as Voice 6 (sober authority). I will hold that."
3. **Diagnostic mode.** The user says "it does not sound like us." Ask them to pick a voice from this list as a quick anchor. Then rewrite.

The fifteen are not exhaustive. They are starting points. A real brand voice is usually a blend, weighted toward one. State the blend explicitly: "70 percent Voice 1 (dry confident), 30 percent Voice 5 (conspirator). The result is dry with a wink."

If the copy is in Spanish, start from Voices 13 to 15 and read `spanish-craft.md` for the variant, the pronoun, and the banned vocabulary before writing a word. An English-tradition voice can be blended in (Voice 13 is cousin to Voice 1), but the conjugation, lexicon, and rhythm come from the regional register, never from translation.

## Voice 1: Dry confident

**Characteristics.** Short sentences. No adjectives. The reader is treated as an adult who can finish the thought. Truth-telling, including against the brand when useful.

**Sample lines.**

> We're number two. We try harder.
> At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise comes from the electric clock.

**Use when.** The brand has a real product story to tell and does not need to dress it up. Strongest for premium products that earn the right to understatement.

**Avoid when.** The product is new and the reader has no reason to trust the dryness yet. Dry confident from an unknown brand reads as cold.

**Brands that have used it well.** Avis (DDB). Volkswagen (DDB classic era). Economist (AMV BBDO). Rolls-Royce (Ogilvy).

---

## Voice 2: Quiet intimate

**Characteristics.** Slower cadence. The reader is addressed as a confidant. Long sentences that earn their length. Specifics drawn from a smaller, sharper world.

**Sample lines.**

> You never actually own a Patek Philippe. You merely look after it for the next generation.
> The men who taught my father how to fix the engine still come to the shop on Saturdays.

**Use when.** Heritage brands, premium goods, anything that wants the reader to slow down. Long copy.

**Avoid when.** B2B SaaS landing pages, anything DTC at a low price point, fast retail. The intimacy reads as fake when the price does not justify the breath.

**Brands that have used it well.** Patek Philippe (Leagas Delaney, Tim Delaney). Saturn (Hal Riney). Macallan whisky.

---

## Voice 3: Manifesto

**Characteristics.** Position before description. Names the enemy. Builds the case. Closes on a stance, not a sell.

**Sample lines.**

> Most people pick the safe job. We made one for the people who could not.
> Here's to the crazy ones, the misfits, the rebels.

**Use when.** Brand launches. Repositioning campaigns. Movements that need to recruit, not just sell. Founders writing the home page they actually believe.

**Avoid when.** Transactional copy. Checkout flows. Anything where the job is to finish a step, not start a belief.

**Brands that have used it well.** Apple (TBWA/Chiat/Day, "Think different"). Nike (Wieden+Kennedy, "Dream Crazy"). Patagonia.

---

## Voice 4: Folksy slow

**Characteristics.** Conversational. Specific. The voice of a small town. The brand as a real person who has time to tell you the story.

**Sample lines.**

> We make cars here in Spring Hill. Some of us have been making them for thirty years.
> The lemonade is made by a man named Frank who has been making lemonade since 1962.

**Use when.** Heritage. Family business. Anything where the labour and the people are the product. Long copy reads beautifully in this voice.

**Avoid when.** Fintech. Anything global. The folksy voice does not scale across cultures and reads as parody if the brand is not actually local.

**Brands that have used it well.** Saturn (Hal Riney). Bartles & Jaymes (Riney). Innocent (in-house, UK).

---

## Voice 5: Conspirator

**Characteristics.** The reader and the brand are on the same side. The category is the enemy. Knowing, slightly winking, occasionally rude.

**Sample lines.**

> Most milk is from cows. Ours is from oats. You can probably do the math on which one was less surprised about it.
> It is just water. Murdered.

**Use when.** Categories full of cynicism that have stopped trying. Challenger brands. Anything where the reader is already half on your side and just needs permission.

**Avoid when.** Public service. Financial services that need to project gravitas. Health and medical.

**Brands that have used it well.** Oatly. Liquid Death. Innocent (in their early years). Cards Against Humanity.

---

## Voice 6: Sober authority

**Characteristics.** Measured. Factual. The brand carries the weight of expertise. Sentences are well constructed but not flashy.

**Sample lines.**

> 14 million British workers do not save enough for retirement. This is what their last decade looks like.
> Tomorrow's edition leads with the central bank's letter. Three reporters spent four months on it.

**Use when.** News. Research. Financial services. Professional services. Anywhere the proof is the product and the voice should not get in the way.

**Avoid when.** Consumer DTC. Anything where the user reads to enjoy, not to be informed.

**Brands that have used it well.** The Economist. Financial Times. The Guardian. Reuters. Bloomberg.

---

## Voice 7: Cheeky rebel

**Characteristics.** Loud. Funny. Confident to the point of arrogance. The brand is in on its own joke. Sentences are short and built for memes.

**Sample lines.**

> Less legroom. Lower fares. Choose your seat.
> For successful living.

**Use when.** Categories where the reader knows the joke about the category and is waiting for someone to make it. Low price brands that own their low price. Brands launching against an entrenched incumbent.

**Avoid when.** Anything that requires building trust over time. The cheeky voice is brittle: one bad campaign and it reads as smug.

**Brands that have used it well.** Spirit Airlines. Ryanair. Diesel ("For Successful Living"). Wendy's (on Twitter).

---

## Voice 8: Founder narrator

**Characteristics.** First person. The story of why the company was built told by the person who built it. Specifics from the founder's actual life. No third person corporate gloss.

**Sample lines.**

> I started this because my dad had to drive 90 minutes to get a part that should have been on the shelf.
> We are 11 people. We answer every email by Tuesday.

**Use when.** Early stage. Indie brands. Anywhere the founder is the brand. About pages. The first paragraph of a sales page where the founder needs to earn credibility.

**Avoid when.** The founder is no longer with the company. The brand has scaled past a single voice. Mature B2B with multiple stakeholders.

**Brands that have used it well.** Basecamp. Stripe (in early Patrick Collison essays). Patagonia (Yvon Chouinard). Innocent's early labels.

---

## Voice 9: Technical precise

**Characteristics.** No marketing words. Specifics from the engineering itself. The voice of the engineer who built the thing and is showing you how it works. Trusts the reader to understand or to ask.

**Sample lines.**

> Payouts arrive in 4 hours instead of 4 days. We rebuilt the settlement layer to do it.
> 0.3 millisecond latency, p99. Anywhere in the EU.

**Use when.** Developer tools. Infrastructure. Hardware. Anything where the buyer is technical and respects unadorned technical fact more than benefit copy.

**Avoid when.** Non technical buyers. Consumer goods. Anything where the reader needs the benefit translated.

**Brands that have used it well.** Stripe (developer-facing). Linear. Vercel. Cloudflare. Anthropic API docs.

---

## Voice 10: Hostile funny

**Characteristics.** The brand attacks the reader's assumption, the category, or the reader's prior choice. Funny, sharp, willing to be unloved by the people it is not for.

**Sample lines.**

> You are probably going to ignore this ad. That is fine. The product still works.
> If your team uses 14 tools to send a single invoice, this is for them.

**Use when.** B2B against entrenched competition. DTC against category fatigue. Anywhere the reader's prior choice is the obstacle to your sale.

**Avoid when.** New categories where the reader has no prior. The voice needs something to push against.

**Brands that have used it well.** Spirit Airlines. Old Spice ("The man your man could smell like"). Some Mailchimp work. Cards Against Humanity.

---

## Voice 11: Plain spoken trader

**Characteristics.** Working class register. Direct. The voice of someone who would rather make the sale and go home than chat. Short. Often funny in spite of itself.

**Sample lines.**

> Does exactly what it says on the tin.
> Should've gone to Specsavers.

**Use when.** Categories where the customer wants no fuss. Hardware stores, plumbing, locksmiths, supermarkets, value retail, regional services.

**Avoid when.** Luxury. Anything where the price requires ceremony to justify.

**Brands that have used it well.** Ronseal (HHCL). Specsavers. Aldi. Lidl. Toolstation.

---

## Voice 12: Public service grave

**Characteristics.** Moral seriousness. Specifics from the cost of the problem. No jokes. No filler. The voice of someone who has seen the consequence and is asking the reader to act.

**Sample lines.**

> Three hundred children went to school this morning who will not come home.
> The water in this village last year was not water.

**Use when.** Charities. Public health. Public safety. Political campaigns. Any moment where the gap between the problem and the reader's awareness is the entire argument.

**Avoid when.** Anything commercial. The voice burns trust if used to sell a product.

**Brands that have used it well.** Amnesty International (Indra Sinha). Sandy Hook Promise. British Heart Foundation. RSPCA (David Abbott).

---

## Voice 13: Porteña seca (rioplatense)

**Characteristics.** Voseo. Understatement. Humour so dry it can pass for a statement of fact. Short clauses, the verb landing late where it hits hardest. The reader is treated as a co-conspirator who already knows the joke about the category. Cousin of Voice 1 (dry confident), but the dryness is porteño: self-aware, slightly fatalistic, never earnest.

**Sample lines.**

> Encontrá lo que buscás.
> Vos firmá tranquilo. Leer la letra chica es nuestro laburo.

**Use when.** Brands speaking to Argentina or Uruguay. Young urban professionals. Categories thick with cynicism (banking, telcos, insurance) where earnestness reads as a lie. Fintech and challenger brands.

**Avoid when.** Pan-LatAm campaigns (the voseo localises instantly). Premium formality (private banking wants usted). Mexico or Spain, where the conjugation alone breaks the spell.

**Register references.** The Agulla & Baccetti era of Quilmes. Mercado Libre's functional voseo. The humour line that runs from Mafalda to Capusotto. Conjugation, lexicon, and rhythm: see `spanish-craft.md`, rioplatense section.

---

## Voice 14: Mexicana directa

**Characteristics.** Tuteo. Fast punchline, short close. Street vocabulary used with precision, one word at a time, never as costume. Warmth without ceremony: the brand talks like the friend who tells you the truth about the price. The Mexican line closes faster than the rioplatense one.

**Sample lines.**

> Ahorita sí: tu cambio de hoy ya está ahorrado.
> No es magia. Es tu lana, mejor acomodada.

**Use when.** Consumer brands in Mexico. Young urban audiences. Categories where corporate coldness is the enemy: banking for the unbanked, delivery, telco challengers.

**Avoid when.** Formal registers (except the ironic usted, which is its own art). Andean or Southern Cone audiences; the lexicon travels badly. Enterprise B2B.

**Register references.** Cerveza Indio's "Diccionario" reclaiming of Mexican Spanish. Telcel's territorial confidence. The punchline tradition that runs from Cantinflas to the meme account. Conjugation and lexicon: see `spanish-craft.md`, mexicano section.

---

## Voice 15: Castellana conceptual

**Characteristics.** Tú and vosotros. The idea play over the dry compression: Spanish advertising's strongest tradition is the concept that rewinds a cliché. The line lands on a thought, not a punch. Comfortable with a longer beat and an emotional close.

**Sample lines.**

> ¿Te gusta conducir?
> El mayor premio es compartirlo.

**Use when.** Brands in Spain. Emotional, concept-led campaigns. TV-led work where the line is the residue of the film. Lottery, automotive, food, telecom.

**Avoid when.** Latin America (vosotros and the rhythm do not travel). Hard direct response, where the concept softens the CTA. Performance formats that need the offer in the first line.

**Register references.** Toni Segarra's "¿Te gusta conducir?" for BMW España. Lotería de Navidad's "El mayor premio es compartirlo." Sra. Rushmore's era of Aquarius. Maestros and cadence: see `spanish-craft.md`, castellano section.

## How to pick a voice in 60 seconds

Ask three questions about the brief.

1. **What is the brand's relationship to the category?** Defender of the category, challenger to it, outsider to it, or above it?
   - Defender often picks Voice 6 (sober authority) or Voice 1 (dry confident).
   - Challenger often picks Voice 5 (conspirator), Voice 7 (cheeky rebel), or Voice 10 (hostile funny).
   - Outsider often picks Voice 3 (manifesto) or Voice 4 (folksy slow).
   - Above the category often picks Voice 2 (quiet intimate).

2. **What is the price point?** Premium, mid, value.
   - Premium gravitates to Voice 2, Voice 6, Voice 1.
   - Mid gravitates to Voice 3, Voice 8, Voice 9.
   - Value gravitates to Voice 11, Voice 7, Voice 5.

3. **Who is the buyer?** Technical, founder, executive, consumer, citizen.
   - Technical wants Voice 9.
   - Founder wants Voice 8 or Voice 3.
   - Consumer wants Voice 4, Voice 5, Voice 7, or Voice 11.
   - Citizen wants Voice 12 or Voice 3.

The three answers narrow the bank to two or three voices. Pick the one most fitting and confirm with the user.

A fourth question when the copy is in Spanish: **which country?** Argentina or Uruguay points to Voice 13. Mexico points to Voice 14. Spain points to Voice 15. Pan-regional campaigns blend Voice 14's tuteo with the register the brief demands, and `spanish-craft.md` settles the pronoun.

## How to write a voice the user has named

If the user names a voice ("write this in our brand voice; here are three samples"):

1. Read the samples aloud.
2. Identify two markers per sample: sentence length, vocabulary register, presence of humour, register of address (formal, intimate, distant), use of specifics, use of metaphor.
3. Match to the closest voice in this bank.
4. Tell the user which voice you matched and what blend ("60 percent Voice 6, 40 percent Voice 9"). Confirm before writing.
5. Hold the voice across every section. A voice drift is the most common reason copy that started well falls apart by the third paragraph.

---

## A working list of voice principles

- Pick the voice before the first word.
- Hold the voice across every section of the copy.
- Read your draft aloud. If a sentence sounds like a different voice, rewrite it.
- A blend of two voices is fine. A blend of four is no voice at all.
- The voice the brand has, not the voice the brand wishes it had. Confirm with the user when in doubt.
